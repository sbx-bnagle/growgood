## Bats are the best

About 15 years ago I spent most nights watching kids, for work, on a rooftop playground in Chicago. At dusk a funnel cloud of bats would come barrelling out of a nearby abandoned smokestack. And every night the kids could care less about the thing of majesty in front of them. Fast forward to current day, and I'm still pointing out bats to kids, my own, who REALLY SHOULD APPRECIATE ME MORE.

A fact I scream to no one in particular: <mark>A single bat can eat thousands of mosquitoes and other insects in a single night.</mark> Again: ONE BAT — THOUSANDS OF MOSQUITOES — EACH NIGHT.

If that&rsquo;s not enough, they&rsquo;re also important pollinators (like hummingbirds), and their droppings make excellent fertilizer (but [not mascara](https://www.snopes.com/fact-check/bat-your-lashes/)). I&rsquo;m hoping to attract them to our garden, but unfortunately they are shy and their numbers are dwindling. They&rsquo;re common in a nearby park though, and I hope we can lure a couple our way.

## Attracting the bats

Here&rsquo;s the plan for getting some bat action: 

- **Reduce light pollution:** admittedly a tall order in an urban area, but we&rsquo;ll do what we can.
- **Plant light colored and stinky flowers:** this attracts bugs that they eat (but not &rsquo;skeeters). Something like evening primrose should be good.
- **Provide water:** we won&rsquo;t be installing anything as dramatic as a pond. This is tough since standing water also attracts the &rsquo;quitoes. Another challenge, bats drink by swooping down and slurping water on the go. To do this they need a length of 7–10ft of water. Good news is they only need to be within a quarter mile of water. If we need to we&rsquo;ll likely go with mosquito dunks or a small solar powered pump to deter the lil&rsquo; &rsquo;skeeties. Will definitely have to do a follow-up post on this.
- **Avoid pesticides:** we avoid pesticides to maintain our smug credentials. Also, they can deter bats.


A couple things that we won&rsquo;t be able to do, but are supposed to help:

- **Add oak trees**: really native trees in general that attract native bugs that the native bats eat.
- **Keep dead trees**: they&rsquo;re great for bats if leaving them up is an option.



## Bat houses (aka a 'bat box')

There are a bunch of cool options for these. But, they are not all created equal. [Some criteria from the US Fish & Wildlife Service](https://www.fws.gov/question-answer/how-and-whether-install-bat-boxes):

*Multi-chamber maternity bat boxes with venting are best for providing bats with a range of microclimates. Avoid small bat houses (the size of a typical bird house) and large single chamber boxes, which may not provide a suitable gradient of temperatures inside, nor enough mass to retain heat at night. If installing a single box, choose a multi-chamber box (four or more) that is taller than it is wide, and includes venting to create cool areas or escape zones that can be used during heat waves.*

We&rsquo;re considering either buying [one like this](https://www.wildlifecontrolsupplies.com/animal/BCBH-4C.html) or [making our own](https://yellowbrickhome.com/make-your-own-bat-box/).

![Bat box, credit: Wildlife Control Supplies](/assets/images/uploads/batbox.jpg)
<small>*Credit: Wildlife Control Supplies*</small>

We also have to find a good spot. The box&rsquo;s placement is almost as important as the the thing itself. Here&rsquo;s the criteria:

- **Right amount of light:** up towards the eaves if on the house/garage on an Eastern wall. Looking for morning sun while not baking them alive in the afternoon.
- **Elevated:** the exit should be 10–16 feet off the ground. 
- **Away from predators:** we&rsquo;re not building a bat buffet.


We&rsquo;ll report back on progress. If you have any tips or tricks please let me know.
